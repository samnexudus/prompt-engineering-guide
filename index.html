Prompt Engineering Training Guide

An interactive guide to writing better AI prompts — covering role, task, context, and constraints.

The four elements of a strong prompt

Well-structured prompts consistently outperform vague ones. Think of them like a brief you'd give a new colleague — the more relevant context you provide, the better the output.

You don't always need all four — a simple factual question rarely needs a Role. But for any complex or creative task, including all four dramatically improves results. Explore each element using the tabs above.

Role
Tell the AI who it is

Assigning a role shapes the AI's perspective, vocabulary, and level of expertise. It's like telling a colleague which hat to wear before they tackle a task.

Professional role"You are an experienced employment solicitor specialising in UK law." — Sets formal, accurate, legally-aware tone.
Skill-based role"You are a data analyst skilled at explaining technical findings to non-technical audiences." — Shifts the communication style, not just the expertise.
Audience-aware role"You are a trainer writing materials for people who have never used software before." — Ensures the output is pitched at the right level.
Tip

You can combine roles: "You are a UX designer with a background in psychology." The more specific the role, the more precisely the output reflects it. But don't over-complicate — one clear role beats three vague ones.

Task
State clearly what needs to be done

The task is the core instruction. Vague tasks produce vague outputs. The more specific and action-oriented your task, the better the result.

Use action verbs"Write", "Summarise", "Compare", "Create", "List", "Rewrite", "Translate", "Draft" — these signal exactly what kind of output you want.
Be specific about the deliverable"Draft a 3-paragraph introduction" is clearer than "write something about our product." Specificity reduces the chance of a mismatch.
Split complex tasksIf you need multiple things, break them into steps or separate prompts. "First summarise, then extract the three key risks" is clearer than one combined ask.
✗ Weak task
"Write something about our new product launch."
✓ Strong task
"Write a 200-word product announcement for our internal Slack channel."
Context
Give the AI the background it needs

Context is what the AI can't guess. Without it, outputs are generic. With it, outputs feel tailored to your exact situation.

Who is the audience?"Our audience is senior managers who are not technical." — Changes vocabulary, depth of explanation, and framing completely.
What's the situation?"We are preparing for a difficult client conversation after a project delay." — Gives the AI the emotional and strategic register to work within.
What's the goal?"The aim is to reassure the client while setting realistic expectations, not to apologise excessively." — This shapes tone, structure, and emphasis.
What to include

Think: who, what, why, and where. You don't need to include everything — only what's genuinely relevant. Irrelevant context can dilute a prompt just as much as missing context.

Constraints
Define the boundaries

Constraints prevent the AI from going off-piste. They cover format, tone, length, what to include — and crucially, what to leave out.

Format"Present this as a numbered list", "Use a two-column table", "Return this as a JSON object", "Write in plain paragraphs with no bullet points."
Tone"Keep it professional but warm", "Avoid corporate jargon", "Write as if speaking to a friend", "Maintain a neutral, factual tone throughout."
Length"Keep this under 150 words", "Aim for 5–7 sentences", "No more than three bullet points per section."
Exclusions"Do not mention pricing", "Avoid making specific legal recommendations", "Don't use the phrase 'game-changer'." — Negative constraints are underused but very effective.
Output format
Tell the AI how to return its answer

Specifying the output format reduces unnecessary tokens, avoids bloated file formats, and makes the result easier to use immediately — especially when feeding output into other tools or workflows.

Prefer plain text or HTML over document formatsAsking for a Word or PDF response forces the AI to describe a formatted document in tokens rather than giving you usable content. Ask for HTML, markdown, or plain text instead — you can always paste or render it yourself.
Use structured formats for machine-readable output"Return your answer as a JSON object with keys: summary, risks, next_steps." This makes output easy to parse, copy, or pipe into another tool without reformatting.
Name the structure, not just the topic"Give me a table with columns: Scenario, Benefit, Risk" is more precise than "compare the options." Named structures eliminate the AI's need to decide how to organise the output.
Ask only for what you'll use"Just the bullet points — no introduction or closing summary." Every sentence the AI writes that you don't need costs tokens and buries the useful content. Cut the wrapping.
Match the format to where it's goingIf the output is going into Slack, ask for plain text. If it's going into a web page, ask for HTML. If it's going into a spreadsheet, ask for comma-separated values. Format at the source — not after the fact.
✗ Wasteful
"Write this up as a Word document with headings and formatting."
✓ Efficient
"Return this as HTML with h2 headings and paragraph tags — I'll paste it directly into our CMS."
Full worked examples

Three complete prompts showing all four elements in action, across different workplace scenarios.

1. Internal communications
You are an experienced internal communications manager. Write an announcement about our upcoming office move to a new building. We are moving from Canary Wharf to Shoreditch in January. The move is partly to reduce costs and partly to be closer to our engineering team. Some employees are concerned about the commute change. Keep the tone positive and honest — acknowledge the change without being dismissive of concerns. Aim for 200 words, and end with a clear call to action (e.g. complete a commute survey).
2. Data analysis
You are a business analyst who specialises in presenting data clearly to non-technical stakeholders. Summarise the key findings from this quarterly sales report and highlight any areas of concern. The audience is our leadership team — they care most about revenue trends, customer retention, and regional performance. They will use this to make budget decisions for Q3. Use plain English throughout. Structure the output as: three key highlights, one area of risk, and one recommended next step. Keep it under 250 words.
3. Training content
You are a learning and development specialist with experience designing onboarding programmes. Create a one-week onboarding plan for a new customer success hire. The role involves managing existing accounts, handling escalations, and running quarterly business reviews. The new hire has general account management experience but is new to SaaS. Structure the plan day by day. Each day should have a theme, two or three activities, and one key goal. Keep the tone warm and practical. Do not include specific tool tutorials — focus on knowledge and relationships.

Common mistakes to avoid

Most poor AI outputs trace back to one of these. Share these with your team as quick wins.

Being too vague"Help me with an email" tells the AI almost nothing. Who is it to? What's the goal? What tone? Always add specifics.
Asking for too many things at onceCombining five unrelated tasks in one prompt dilutes quality. Separate distinct tasks into separate prompts.
Accepting the first output without iterationTreat the first response as a draft. Follow up: "Make this more concise", "Change the tone to be less formal", "Add a stronger opening line."
Forgetting to say what NOT to doExclusion constraints are powerful. "Don't use bullet points", "Avoid mentioning competitors by name", "Don't assume prior knowledge."
Omitting the audienceWho is this for? A response aimed at a technical developer reads very differently from one aimed at a first-time user. Always state who will read or use the output.
